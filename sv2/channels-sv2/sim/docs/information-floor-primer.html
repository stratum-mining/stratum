The Information Floor

Why a hashrate estimate can only be so precise — and why setting difficulty too high starves the very signal you'd need to notice.

By Eric Price · Senior Software Engineer, MARA

A ground-up primer on the estimation theory behind information-floor.md. No prior statistics assumed; the plain-English essays assume none of this.

What this is for

The two essays make an argument without the math: more shares means a sharper estimate, there's a limit, and the only real lever is the share rate. That's all a reader needs to follow the why.

The technical report proves it, and the proof rests on one result from estimation theory: the Cramér–Rao lower bound, which gives the estimate's precision a hard floor of 1/√(r*τ). This primer builds that result from nothing, so the report's central claim reads as a conclusion you understand rather than a formula you take on faith.

You almost certainly already have the intuition for the first half. What you may not have are the names — Fisher information, the Cramér–Rao bound — and the one line of algebra that turns the intuition into the exact 1/√(r*τ). That's the gap this closes.

Then a second, harder idea most people don't intuit: what happens when the difficulty is set wrong. Chapter 2 builds the collapse r_obs/r* = exp(−e) from the ground up — the relation that makes over-difficulty self-reinforcing — and shows why it forces the whole design to treat "too hard" and "too easy" as opposites rather than mirror images. If exp(−e) looks opaque right now, that's exactly what Chapter 2 is for.

Chapter 1

The floor

How precisely can you measure a miner's hashrate from its shares? There's a hard limit, it's set by how much data you have, and no cleverness beats it.

Step 1

Estimating a rate by counting

A miner's hashrate isn't something the pool measures directly. It's something the pool infers — from the shares the miner submits.

A share is a proof-of-work hit: the miner is grinding through hashes, and every so often one clears the difficulty target and becomes a share. The higher the hashrate, the more often that happens. So the pool watches the share stream and works backward: this many shares in this much time, at this difficulty, implies roughly this hashrate.

Strip away the mining specifics and the shape of the problem is old and well-understood: you are estimating a rate by counting random events over a window of time. The same shape shows up in counting clicks from a Geiger counter, calls arriving at a call center, or raindrops hitting a sensor. In all of them you have events that arrive at some true average rate, you count how many land in a fixed window, and you use that count to estimate the rate.

The key fact about these events — and the one everything below depends on — is that each hash is an independent trial. A hash either clears the target or it doesn't, the miner does astronomically many of them, and whether any one clears is independent of the others. Events like that arrive in a specific statistical pattern called a Poisson process, and a Poisson process has one property we'll lean on hard: over a window, the number of events you see has an average, and it has a spread around that average — and the spread is completely determined by the average.

Why "Poisson" matters later When the essays' companion analysis says share arrivals stay honest and independent, this is the claim: hashing is a Poisson process. That's what lets us pin down the noise exactly, rather than hand-wave it. Hold onto the word; the report uses it.

Step 2

Why the estimate is noisy — and exactly how noisy

Here's the fact that does the most work in the whole subject, and it's the one you already know from running miners: an estimate off a few shares bounces around; an estimate off many shares is steady.

You've watched this happen. Point a fresh miner at a pool and the estimated hashrate is all over the place for the first little while — then it settles as the shares pile up. Nothing about the miner changed; the estimate got better because it had more to go on. That settling is the entire phenomenon, and now we're going to say precisely how much it settles.

For a Poisson process, if you expect N events in your window on average, the spread — the typical amount your actual count differs from N — is √N. Not N, not N²: the square root of N. This is the one non-obvious fact you have to take from the mathematics, and it's worth stating on its own:

count in window  ≈  N  ±  √N
# expected count N, typical wobble √N

Now watch what that does to the relative error — the wobble as a fraction of the thing you're measuring, which is what actually matters. The absolute wobble is √N, so as a fraction of N it's:

relative error  =  √N / N  =  1 / √N
# the wobble shrinks, but only as 1 over the square root of the count

There's the square-root law, and there's why it's a law of diminishing returns. Collect 4× as many shares and the relative error doesn't drop to a quarter — it drops by √4 = 2, i.e. it halves. To halve the error again you need 4× more still. Every further factor of two in precision costs four times the shares. Precision is buyable, but the price climbs steeply.

The estimate improves as the square root of the count. That's not a mining fact — it's what counting random events forces on you.

Work it out, then open

You want to halve the error, and error goes as 1/√N. Halving the error means doubling √N, which means quadrupling N — so about 400 shares. (Check: 1/√400 = 1/20 = 5%.) The jump from 100→400 to buy one halving is the diminishing return made concrete.

Step 3

Information, named: the Fisher information

We've been saying "more shares means a better estimate." Estimation theory gives the "more to go on" a precise name and a number: the Fisher information.

The idea is exactly what it sounds like. Every observation you make carries some information about the quantity you're trying to pin down. The Fisher information is a measurement of how much a set of observations tells you about an unknown — a bigger number means the data pins the answer down more tightly, a smaller number means the data leaves it fuzzier.

You don't need the defining formula to use the concept, and this primer won't derive it — the report can, if you want it. What you need is the one structural property that makes it the right tool, and it's the property you'd hope for: for independent observations, Fisher information adds up. Two independent shares carry twice the information of one; a hundred carry a hundred times. Information accumulates as you collect data, one observation at a time.

For our counting problem, that means the Fisher information about the rate grows with the number of events you observe. More shares, more information — now stated in the vocabulary the technical report uses. And that sets up the punchline, because information and precision are about to turn out to be two views of the same thing.

The name, so it's not a surprise later "Fisher information" (after the statistician R. A. Fisher) is just the formal name for how much the data tells you. When the report writes it, it means the thing you already understood as "how many shares you have to go on."

Step 4

The floor: the Cramér–Rao bound

Now the result the whole report stands on — and, again, one you half-know: no matter how clever your formula, you can't be more precise than your data allows.

You already believe this. You know you can't take a jittery ten-share estimate and, by processing it more cleverly, turn it into the steady estimate you'd get from a thousand shares. The information isn't there; no amount of arithmetic conjures it. The Cramér–Rao lower bound is that belief made exact. It says:

variance of any unbiased estimate  ≥  1 / (Fisher information)
# "variance" = the spread, squared. More information → smaller possible spread.

Read it in words: the spread of your estimate can't be smaller than one over the information you have. The information sets a floor on the achievable error, and the floor is not something a better algorithm gets under — it's the best any method can do with that data. Beating it isn't hard; it's impossible, the way a perpetual-motion machine is impossible, not the way a hard problem is hard.

This is why "use a smarter controller to track hashrate more precisely" is a dead end, and the report can say so with a theorem rather than an opinion. All the reasonable estimators cluster just above this floor because the floor is where the information runs out. The differences between them are rounding error next to the wall they're all pressed against.

The floor isn't the best we've found. It's the best there is — a limit on what the data can support, not on our ingenuity.
One honest qualifier The bound is stated for unbiased estimators — ones that don't systematically run high or low. That's the right frame here (you want an honest hashrate estimate, not one skewed on purpose), but it's a real condition, not decoration. A biased estimator can trade some honesty for less spread; the report is careful about this, and so should you be. "No estimator beats it" is shorthand for "no unbiased estimator beats it."

Step 5

Putting it together: where 1/√(r*τ) comes from

Everything you need is now on the table. The report's formula is three steps you've already taken, stacked.

Start from the count. Your window is some length of time — call it τ (the estimator window). Shares arrive at the true rate — call it r* (shares per unit time). So the number of shares you expect to see in the window is just rate × time:

N  =  r* × τ
# expected shares = share rate times window length

Now apply Step 2's square-root law. The relative error of a count-based rate estimate is 1/√N. Substitute N = r*τ:

relative error  =  1 / √N  =  1 / √(r* τ)
# the floor, in the report's own notation

That's the whole formula. It isn't an arbitrary expression to memorize — it's "count random events, and your relative error is one over the square root of how many you counted," with the count written as rate times window. And read through the Fisher-information lens of Steps 3–4, the story closes: more shares (larger r*τ) means more information, which by Cramér–Rao means a lower floor on the error. Precision, information, and count are one thing seen three ways.

Check yourself

Two knobs sit inside r*τ: the share rate r* and the window τ. Suppose you double the window τ (average over twice as long) instead of raising the rate. What happens to the floor — and what does it cost you?

Think it through, then open

Doubling τ doubles r*τ exactly as doubling r* would, so the floor drops by the same √2 — mathematically the two knobs look identical. The difference is what they cost. A longer window buys precision by making the estimate slower: you're averaging over more history, so you react to a real hashrate change later. Raising the rate buys the same precision without that lag. That trade — precision vs. responsiveness — is exactly why the window isn't a free lever, and it's a thread the report picks up.

Step 6

Why there's exactly one lever

This is the payoff the essays assert and the report proves — and now you can see the machinery under it.

The floor is 1/√(r*τ), and the Cramér–Rao bound says no estimator gets under it. So ask: what can an engineer actually change to make tracking more precise?

Not the estimator. Every unbiased estimator is already at or just above the floor — that's Step 4. Polishing the formula moves you from "just above the floor" to "at the floor," a rounding-error gain. There's no room there.

The window τ — but at a price. A longer window lowers the floor, but by making the estimate slower to react (Step 5's check). You can trade responsiveness for precision, but you haven't lowered the fundamental cost of information; you've just spent time to buy it.

The rate r* — the real lever. Raising the share rate raises r*τ, which raises the information, which lowers the floor — without the lag the window imposes. It genuinely lowers the wall everyone is pressed against. In mining terms, more shares per unit time is more information per unit time, and that's the one move that improves the whole thing rather than trading one virtue for another.

The estimator can't beat the floor and the window only trades time for precision. Raising the share rate is the one move that lowers the floor itself.

That is why the essays say the share rate is the only real lever, and why the report can defend it as a consequence of the Cramér–Rao bound rather than a design preference. There's one place the information comes from, and turning that knob is the only thing that adds information for free.

Step 7

What the floor does — and does not — say

A primer earns trust by marking its own edges. Two, before you go to the report.

The floor is about tracking precision, not about every cost in the system

The Cramér–Rao floor tells you how precisely you can estimate a hashrate from shares. That's one dimension of the vardiff problem — an important one — but not the whole of it. The report separates several distinct costs, and confusing "the estimate is imprecise" with "the controller is behaving badly" is exactly the kind of category error the report is careful to avoid. Read the floor as what it is: a limit on estimation, which is one input to the larger question of controller behavior, not the whole answer.

The square-root law assumes independent, Poisson arrivals

The clean √N spread is exactly right for independent events. Real share streams can depart from that — arrivals can clump for reasons of hardware timing, network batching, or a control loop that correlates one window to the next — and when they do, the real spread is wider than the idealized 1/√(r*τ). The floor is a floor: it's the best case, the limit you approach when the arrivals are as clean as the model assumes. The report is explicit that reality sits at or above it, never below. Treat 1/√(r*τ) as the best achievable, and expect the field to be a bit worse.

Why this matters for what you read next A tempting but wrong story says the harm from a bad controller is "lost information" from clumped shares. That's a real phenomenon — but it is not where the report's headline cost comes from, and the report is careful to keep them apart. Carry the distinction in: the floor is about how much the data can tell you; a controller sitting at the wrong difficulty is a different problem with a different cost.

End of Chapter 1 — and the setup for Chapter 2

You now have the three names Chapter 1 was built to give you: the Poisson arrival process, the Fisher information that grows with the shares you collect, and the Cramér–Rao bound that turns that information into a hard floor 1/√(r*τ). Precision depends on the share rate. Hold that — Chapter 2 needs it.

Chapter 1 assumed the difficulty was set roughly right and asked how precisely you could measure. Chapter 2 asks the opposite question: what happens when the difficulty is set wrong? The answer is where the real danger lives — and it turns on a single relation, r_obs/r* = exp(−e), that we'll build one piece at a time.

Chapter 2

The collapse

Set difficulty too high and you don't just get a wrong number — you choke off the shares you'd need to notice. That feedback is the whole ballgame, and it makes "too hard" and "too easy" fundamentally different.

Where this chapter is headed

Everything below builds toward one loop: an over-difficulty error starves the shares the controller needs to detect the error, so the error protects itself. That self-reinforcing loop is the death spiral, and it's the reason a declining miner can get stranded at too-high difficulty.

We build the loop from parts, bottom-up, so that when it assembles (Step 11) every link is something you've already proved rather than taken on faith. The parts, in order: a clean way to measure difficulty error (Step 8), the relation that turns error into a collapsing share rate (Step 9, the crux), and the formula tying a hashrate drop to an exact error (Step 10). Hold the loop as the destination; the steps are how we earn it.

Step 8

Measuring error in the right units

Before we can talk about difficulty being "wrong," we need a clean way to measure how wrong. That measure is a logarithm, and the reason is worth getting exactly, because everything in this chapter is written in it.

The controller doesn't add or subtract difficulty — it multiplies it. It decides the miner should be at half the current difficulty, or twice, or 1.2×. Difficulty moves by factors. So the honest way to ask "how wrong is the current setting" is also multiplicative: by what factor is our estimate of the hashrate off from the truth?

Call the controller's current estimate of the hashrate Ĥ ("H-hat" — the belief it's acting on) and the true hashrate H. The natural measure of wrongness is the ratio Ĥ/H — how many times off we are. If Ĥ/H = 2, the controller thinks the miner is twice as fast as it really is (so it sets difficulty too high). If Ĥ/H = 0.5, it thinks the miner is half as fast (difficulty too low).

But a raw ratio is an awkward ruler, because it's not symmetric around "correct." Being off by 2× too high gives a ratio of 2; being off by 2× too low gives 0.5. Those are the *same size* of mistake in opposite directions — but 2 and 0.5 aren't the same distance from 1. The ruler is stretched on one side and squashed on the other. Taking the logarithm fixes exactly this. Define the error as

e  =  ln( Ĥ / H )
# the log of the ratio — "how many factors off, as a signed distance"

Now the ruler is symmetric. A logarithm turns "2× too high" into ln 2 ≈ +0.69 and "2× too low" into ln 0.5 ≈ −0.69 — the same distance from zero, just opposite signs. Reading it off:

e = 0  →  exact (Ĥ = H)
e > 0  →  over-difficulty (estimate too high → difficulty too hard)
e < 0  →  under-difficulty (estimate too low → difficulty too easy)

That's the whole idea of Step 8: e is just "how wrong, measured multiplicatively, on a ruler where equal factors are equal distances." It's a signed number, zero when perfect, positive when too hard, negative when too easy. Everything below is written in e for one reason — it's the only ruler on which the two directions are comparable, which we're about to need, because they turn out not to be comparable in consequence at all.

Check yourself

The controller's estimate is 25% too high (Ĥ/H = 1.25). Is e positive or negative, and roughly what does the "too high" mean the miner is experiencing — difficulty too hard, or too easy?

Answer

e = ln(1.25) ≈ +0.22 — positive, because the estimate is above the truth. And "estimate too high" means the controller thinks the miner is faster than it is, so it sets the difficulty bar too hard for what the miner can actually do. Positive e = over-difficulty = the dangerous direction, as the next step will show.

Step 9 · the center of the whole primer

The collapse: why the observed rate is exp(−e)

This is the step to slow down on. It's the relation you said looked opaque, and by the end of it the opacity should be gone — because it's really just one physical fact plus the log ruler from Step 8.

The physical fact

When difficulty is set too high, shares come in less often. That's not subtle — it's the definition of difficulty. A share is a hash that clears the target; raise the target (harder difficulty) and fewer hashes clear it, so shares arrive more slowly. The miner is working exactly as hard as before; the pool just sees fewer shares, because each one now takes more work to produce.

So there are two share rates to keep apart:

r*
the rate shares would arrive at if difficulty were set correctly
r_obs
the rate the pool actually observes, at the current (possibly wrong) difficulty

When difficulty is correct, r_obs = r*. When it's too hard, r_obs < r* — you observe fewer than you should. The question is: by exactly how much?

Turning "how much less" into a formula

Here's the one link to make. Difficulty is proportional to the estimate Ĥ (the controller sets the bar based on what it thinks the hashrate is). And share rate is inversely proportional to difficulty (double the difficulty, halve the share rate — each share takes twice the work). Put those together: the observed rate is the true rate scaled by how many times too high the estimate is — i.e. divided by the ratio Ĥ/H:

r_obs / r*  =  1 / (Ĥ/H)  =  H/Ĥ
# observed rate falls in proportion to how much too high the difficulty is set

That's already the whole physical content: if the difficulty is set 2× too high (Ĥ/H = 2), you observe half the shares (r_obs/r* = 1/2). Now the only remaining move is to rewrite H/Ĥ in the log ruler from Step 8. Recall e = ln(Ĥ/H). Then Ĥ/H = exp(e) — here exp(·) is the exponential function, the exact inverse of the ln we used to define e, so exp and ln undo each other. That gives H/Ĥ = exp(−e). Substitute:

r_obs / r*  =  H/Ĥ  =  exp(−e)
# the collapse — observed rate as a function of the error e

That's it. exp(−e) isn't a mysterious object — it's just "H/Ĥ (observed rate falls in proportion to over-setting) written in the log units where the error is called e." The exponential is there only because we chose to measure error as a logarithm in Step 8; exp(−e) is the log ruler and the physical fact, snapped together.

What the shape tells you — and why it matters

Now read the curve. The whole point of writing it as exp(−e) is that the shape of the exponential carries the danger:

0%

Drag the top slider into over-difficulty (e > 0) and watch the observed rate collapse; drag left and it floods above 100%. Or drag the bottom slider — a hashrate drop — and watch it land you at a point on the same curve: that's the closed form e = −ln(1−d) of Step 10, which is just where on this collapse a drop puts you.

To the right (over-difficulty, e > 0): the rate collapses toward zero. And collapsing shares is precisely what Chapter 1 told you destroys precision — remember 1/√(r*τ), the floor that gets worse as the rate drops. So over-difficulty does something vicious: it starves the share stream, and the starved share stream is exactly the signal the controller needs to notice it's over-difficult. The error suppresses the evidence of the error. That is the death spiral, and now you can see it isn't a metaphor — it's the exp(−e) curve feeding back into Chapter 1's floor. Too hard → fewer shares → worse estimate → stays too hard.

To the left (under-difficulty, e < 0): the rate rises above 100%. Set difficulty too easy and shares flood in — more than the true rate. By the same Chapter 1 logic, more shares means a sharper estimate, faster. So under-difficulty is self-correcting: being too easy floods you with the very signal that reveals you're too easy, and you fix it quickly.

Over-difficulty starves the signal that would catch it. Under-difficulty floods you with it. The same curve makes one direction a trap and the other self-healing.

Check yourself

This is the crux, so take a second with it: why is over-difficulty more dangerous than under-difficulty by the same factor? Not "because the formula is asymmetric" — say it in terms of shares and detection.

Say it in your own words first, then open

Because of what each direction does to the shares you need to see the error. Over-difficulty (e > 0) drives the observed rate down (exp(−e) shrinks), so you get fewer shares — and fewer shares means a worse estimate (Chapter 1's floor), so you're slower to notice and correct. It's self-reinforcing. Under-difficulty (e < 0) drives the rate up, so you get more shares, a sharper estimate, faster correction. It's self-limiting. Same size of mistake, opposite feedback: one hides itself, the other reveals itself. That's why the two directions can't be treated as mirror images — and it's the whole reason the report's controller eases eagerly but tightens reluctantly.

Step 10

Drop depth, exactly: e = −ln(1−d)

With e and exp(−e) in hand, one more short step ties the abstract error to a concrete event you can name: a miner losing hashrate.

Suppose a miner is running fine — difficulty set correctly, e = 0 — and then it drops: a hashboard fails, it throttles, it curtails, and its true hashrate falls to a fraction (1−d) of what it was. Here d is the depth of the drop: d = 0.10 is a 10% loss, d = 0.5 is losing half, d = 0.8 is losing 80%.

If the controller doesn't react — its estimate Ĥ stays where it was while the truth H fell — then the error is no longer zero. Plug the new truth into e = ln(Ĥ/H):

e  =  ln( Ĥ / (1−d)H )  =  ln( 1/(1−d) )  =  −ln(1−d)
# the steady over-difficulty error a drop of depth d parks you at

(Using Ĥ = H from before the drop, so Ĥ/H becomes 1/(1−d).) That's the closed form: an undetected drop of depth d puts the controller at a steady over-difficulty of exactly −ln(1−d) — a clean formula, no simulation, tying a physical event to an exact error. A 10% drop parks you at −ln(0.9) ≈ +0.105; a 50% drop at −ln(0.5) ≈ +0.69; an 80% drop at −ln(0.2) ≈ +1.61.

And notice which direction it is: a drop the controller doesn't follow leaves you at positive e — over-difficulty, the dangerous side of Step 9's curve. A miner that loses hashrate and gets left at its old difficulty is, by this formula, pushed straight onto the self-reinforcing branch. That is why a decline is the scenario the whole report is organized around: it's the event that plants the controller on the spiral side.

A small expansion worth seeing For a small drop, −ln(1−d) ≈ d (e.g. a 10% drop gives ≈ 0.105, barely more than 0.10). So for small declines the error is about equal to the drop depth — which sounds harmless, and is exactly the trap the first implication below springs.

Step 11

Three things that follow

The machinery is built. Here is what it forces — three consequences, each one now something you can derive rather than accept.

1 · The cost of a difficulty error is linear, not squared

How should you penalize being wrong? The instinctive statistical choice is squared error — cost proportional to e². It's wrong here, and the closed form shows why. A persistent drop of depth d sits at e = −ln(1−d) ≈ d. Penalize it as e² and a small drop costs ≈ d² — which vanishes as the drop shrinks: a 10% leak costs 0.01, a 3% leak costs 0.001, nearly nothing. So a cost function built on e² says small persistent leaks are almost free — and an algorithm that ignores a slowly-failing miner scores better under e² than one that catches it. That's backwards: the operational harm (lost or wasted work) scales with the magnitude d, linearly. So the honest cost norm is |e|, not e² — and this isn't taste, it's forced by the fact that −ln(1−d) ≈ d. The squared norm has a blind spot exactly where operators care most (the small, chronic, undetected leak), and the closed form is what exposes it.

2 · The death spiral, assembled

This is where the two chapters lock together — and it's worth walking each link, because the spiral isn't one fact, it's a loop where every step feeds the next. You have two mechanisms in hand:

From Chapter 1: the controller's estimate can only be as precise as the shares allow — 1/√(r*τ). Fewer shares in the window means a fuzzier estimate. And the estimate is the only thing the controller has: it can't see the miner directly, it can only infer the hashrate from the share stream (that was Step 1). So a fuzzy estimate means the controller is genuinely uncertain what the hashrate is.

From Chapter 2, Step 9: when difficulty is set too high (e > 0), the observed share rate collapses as exp(−e) — you get fewer shares than a correctly-set difficulty would produce.

Now watch them feed each other, one arrow at a time:

1. difficulty too high (e>0)
2. → observed share rate collapses (Step 9: exp(−e) ↓)
3. → fewer shares in the window
4. → the estimate gets fuzzier (Chapter 1: precision needs shares)
5. → the controller is less able to tell how over-difficult it is
6. → harder to correct — the error protects itself
7. → back to step 1

Read the arrows out loud and each one is a mechanism you already proved. Step 2 is the collapse; step 4 is the floor. The loop closes because correcting an over-difficulty error requires noticing it, noticing requires a decent estimate, and a decent estimate requires shares — which the error itself has choked off. The controller isn't stubborn; the error is erasing the evidence of the error. And Step 10 is what plants you here: a hashrate drop the controller doesn't follow puts it at e = −ln(1−d) > 0, at the top of this loop. The drop is the push; exp(−e) is what makes the push tend to sustain itself.

Correcting the error needs shares; the error destroys the shares. That circular dependency — not stubbornness — is the spiral, and it's just Chapter 1 and Chapter 2 chained.

3 · The asymmetry — why "ease" and "tighten" aren't mirror images

The same exp(−e) that makes over-difficulty a spiral makes under-difficulty self-healing (Step 9's two branches). So the two directions have opposite risk: acting wrongly toward too-hard can spiral; acting wrongly toward too-easy self-corrects via the share flood. A controller that understands this should therefore be eager to ease (the safe direction — if wrong, it self-corrects) and reluctant to tighten (the dangerous direction — if wrong, it can spiral). That policy — eager-ease / reluctant-tighten — is the report's central design principle, and you can now see it isn't a heuristic someone picked. It's forced by the shape of exp(−e): the physics of how difficulty error feeds back on the share stream makes the two directions genuinely different, so the correct controller treats them differently.

Ease eagerly, tighten reluctantly — not as a rule of thumb, but because exp(−e) makes one direction self-healing and the other self-reinforcing.

Step 12

The dynamics — who actually escapes

The three consequences are static — they describe the landscape. But the spiral raised a question the closed forms can't answer on their own, and it's worth facing squarely before the report does it rigorously.

Step 11's loop says over-difficulty is a region where corrections get noisy and wrong moves self-punish — the terrain is tilted against escape. But "tilted against escape" is not the same as "no escape." A fuzzy estimate isn't silent; it's noisy — the controller keeps correcting, just on unreliable readings, so a bad reading can push it the wrong way (tighten when it should ease) and a good one can pull it out. Whether that noisy process escapes the tilt or gets trapped by it depends on how the noise size, the correction step, and the controller's speed interact over many iterations — a dynamical question the closed forms don't settle. The static machinery gives the terrain; the dynamics decide who gets out.

That question is exactly what the report's decline-safety simulations resolve — and the answer is that it depends on the controller. Here it is as a moving picture. Same terrain, opposite fate: the gentle controller escapes the tilt; the twitchy one is trapped by it. That is the escape-vs-trap result — and it's the dynamical confirmation of why consequence 3 holds, because easing is the direction that steers a controller's own state back toward the sharp-vision region.

Notice too what the left side shows: both controllers recover from under-difficulty. There is no mirror-image trap on the safe side — that absence is the asymmetry made visible. The dangerous direction has a trap only the gentle controller escapes; the safe direction has only fast self-correction, for everyone.

Two honest caveats on the safe side The under-difficulty (left) side is self-correcting, not free: while it lasts, the connection runs permanently over its target share rate (extra bandwidth, CPU, share-accounting) — bounding exactly that standing load is why the target share rate is set where it is. And the clean "over-difficulty traps, under-difficulty self-corrects" story is the dense-rate regime: at very sparse share rates the report finds the protective mechanism becomes symmetric instead (there aren't enough shares for the flood to sharpen anything quickly). The report is precise about both; the cloud shows the dense-regime asymmetry, the common and load-bearing case.

Where this is exact, and where it idealizes

Same honesty as Chapter 1. The collapse r_obs/r* = exp(−e) and the closed form e = −ln(1−d) are exact within the report's model — which assumes, among other things, that the true hashrate holds steady within a measurement window and that each connection is one worker. The report states these assumptions explicitly. They're the idealizations that make the formulas clean; reality is messier at the edges (a hashrate that's itself moving during the window, pooled workers), and the report is careful about where those matter. And the cloud above is a calibrated illustration of the dynamics, not the production model — its settled values match the report's, but the rigorous run is slow-decline.rs. Carry the results in as the model's exact behavior — which is what makes the spiral and the asymmetry real mechanisms rather than hand-waving — while knowing where the model's boundaries, and this primer's illustrations, give way to the report's measurements.
