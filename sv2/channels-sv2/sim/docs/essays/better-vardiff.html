Companion essay — the why

A better vardiff, and what “better” turned out to mean

We set out to build a controller that tracks a miner more tightly. We ended up somewhere we didn't expect.

By Eric Price · Senior Software Engineer, Mara

Part one of a two-part article, in plain language. This is the why — the story of what we found; part two, “Building a vardiff, part by part,” is the how. There's also a technical report, “Vardiff at the Information Floor,” with the full derivations and proofs — there if you want them, but you don't need it to follow either part.

The short version

We set out to build a vardiff that tracks a miner more tightly than the one we had. Three things we didn't expect:

They're all about the same. Across the share rates that matter, every reasonable vardiff comes out about even on overall cost — pinned to a precision floor no cleverness beats. (Pick one narrow axis and you can spread the field wide; it's the combined cost that flattens.) So “better” couldn't mean “more accurate.”

The whole game is the decline. What separates controllers is whether they spiral on a falling miner — lagging the drop, holding the difficulty too high, and choking off the very shares needed to notice. Declines are constant — heat, failing boards, grid curtailment — so that's the common case, not a corner one. The controller we ship is the calmest one that never spirals.

There's one real lever. The only way to buy more precision is to ask the miner for more shares per minute. Everything else just trades one weakness for another.

01 — where we started

The job, and the controller we had

A mining pool serves machines that range from a hobbyist's box to an industrial farm — a millionfold spread in power. But it wants every connection to send back proof-of-work “shares” at about the same steady rate, a few per minute. Too few and the pool can't tell what a miner is doing; too many and it drowns in messages.

The tool for this is variable difficulty, or vardiff: give each connection its own difficulty target and keep nudging it so the miner's share rate stays near a chosen rate. The difficulty rises for big miners and falls for small ones, so they all report in at the same comfortable pace.

The controller we had — call it the classic one — is simple and dependable: it checks in on a fixed schedule and steps the difficulty by fixed amounts. It has worked fine for years. Our goal was modest and obvious: build something that tracks a miner's hashrate better — reacts faster, sits closer to target. Find the best controller. We wrote a batch of candidates and a way to score them, fully expecting a clear winner to emerge.

That expectation — that there's a meaningful gap between a good vardiff and a mediocre one — is the thing that turned out to be wrong.

02 — how vardiffs differ

A handful of dials

Strip away the details and every vardiff is the same few dials, set differently:

How long it waits before trusting what it sees (a long averaging window is steady but slow; a short one is jumpy but quick). How far off the rate has to drift before it acts. How big a step it takes when it does. And how often it's allowed to move at all.

Those four dials really do just three jobs: measuring the rate (the waiting window), deciding when a drift is real enough to act on (the threshold), and moving the difficulty when it is (the step, and how often). What separates one vardiff from another is mostly how much care goes into each — and, as we'll see, whether it treats them as separate jobs at all. (Turning these three jobs into three parts you actually build — and deciding what belongs in each — is the whole subject of the companion piece, “Building a vardiff, part by part.”)

Turn those dials one way and you get a twitchy controller that reacts to every flicker. Turn them the other way and you get a sleepy one that stays calm and moves rarely. Intuition says the right settings somewhere in between give you a clearly better tracker. So we went looking for them.

It helps to have a real one in mind. Take ckpool — a mature, well-regarded pool many operators know. It's built from exactly these four dials, with unusual care poured into the waiting-window in particular: not one averaging window but several at once, with logic to lean on the fast one when shares are pouring in and the slow one when they're sparse. It's the kind of carefully-engineered estimator you'd expect to simply track better than a plain one. Keep it in mind — we'll come back to how it does.

03 — the wall

The controller is flying blind

Here is the fact that changes everything. A vardiff never sees the miner. It can't read the hashrate off a gauge. All it ever gets is shares arriving — and shares come at random, like clicks on a Geiger counter. A miner averaging six shares a minute might send four this minute and nine the next, purely by chance.

So to tell a real slowdown from an unlucky stretch, the controller has to average over time. And that's where the wall is: the precision you can get out of that average is capped. It improves only with more shares to average over — and with diminishing returns, so buying real precision takes a lot more of them. There is a floor on the noise, and no algorithm, however clever, gets under it.

The first thing that floor does is set a trap. Average longer and you get more precise — but you're also slower to notice a real change, because you're leaning on more old data. Precision and speed come out of one fixed budget. You cannot have both at once.

04 — the surprise

They're all about the same

Because that floor is set by the share rate and the window — not by how clever the algorithm is — something surprising falls out when you score the whole field. Every reasonable vardiff lands within about 12% of every other one, measured in overall cost. The elaborate ones and the plain ones pile up against the same wall.

Remember ckpool and its several blended windows — the estimator you'd expect to pull ahead? It lands in the same band as the plainest single-window controller. The care went into precision the floor had already capped; it never became a better track.

That broke our original framing. We'd gone looking for “the best tracker,” and there barely is one. The differences we'd been chasing — this window versus that threshold — mostly wash out, because they're all bumping against the same limit. The gap we assumed was there isn't.

Which raises the real question: if they all come out about even on cost, what is actually worth choosing between them?

05 — the exception

One knob that wasn't a wash

The honest first answer was discouraging: almost nothing. The controllers really are that close. But almost nothing isn't nothing — and the one difference that survived the dead heat is the loose thread the rest of this story pulls on. When we stopped ranking controllers by their headline cost number and asked a blunter question — which ones actually drove a miner into trouble — the field stopped looking flat. The controllers that moved difficulty asymmetrically — quick to ease it down, slow to push it back up — rode out declines that toppled the ones that adjusted evenly. Averaged into the overall cost it barely showed. Among the runs that went wrong, it was nearly the whole difference.

That was strange, because on paper the two directions look identical. Difficulty is just a number; nudging it up a notch or down a notch by the same amount ought to cost the same. So we went looking for where the asymmetry was earning its keep, and reached for the obvious explanation first. A controller that's slow to raise difficulty — or quick to lower it — spends less time holding the bar too high; and a bar held too high, surely, is rejecting shares the miner worked for and should have been paid for. By that reasoning the asymmetry was just a way to throw away less: ease fast, reject less, lose less.

The asymmetry plainly helped. The reason we first reached for — that it was saving wasted work — turned out to be a red herring.

It's a tidy story, and it's wrong — wrong in a way we could check directly, and did. There was no wasted work to save; we'll come back to that, with the receipts. But running it down — working out what the asymmetry was actually doing — meant turning to the one thing the shootout had glossed over. Not how a controller tracks a healthy miner holding steady, but what happens to a miner as it comes apart. That, it turns out, is where all the danger lives.

06 — why decline is the danger

Miners don't fail all at once

To see what actually separates one controller from another, look at how a miner gets into trouble in the first place. It almost never drops dead in one step. It loses ground gradually, partially, and silently — and that shape is the whole reason one direction of error is dangerous and the other isn't.

The most common cause is just heat. As a machine or the room around it warms up, its chips quietly throttle themselves to stay safe — the single largest source of gradual hashrate loss, and it trips no alarm. You don't lose the miner; you lose 10 to 30% of it, drifting with the temperature over minutes and hours.

Hardware wears the same way. Boards degrade in increments, firmware routes around dead chips, and a single failed board on a three-board machine walks off with about a third of the hashrate. The clean, total failure is the rare case. The slow bleed is the normal one.

And the largest deliberate reductions come from the grid. A site on a demand-response contract can power its machines all the way off — a clean shutdown the controller handles easily, because the connection simply closes. But it can also curtail partially, holding the fleet at a reduced power target: every machine underclocked to a fraction of its output, hashing less with the connection still live. For a grid-scale operation that partial mode is routine — a large, deliberate hashrate drop on a connection that stays up the whole time.

So the decline a vardiff actually has to survive is rarely a cliff. It's a gentle, partial slide on a connection that stays up — which, as it happens, is the exact shape a slow controller handles worst.

07 — the trap

The death spiral

If you know Bitcoin's “death spiral,” set that aside — that's the network-level worry that a price crash sheds hashrate faster than difficulty can retarget down, and it's mostly dismissed, because the retarget does catch up. This is a different animal: one connection's vardiff, where the loop is real and plays out in minutes.

Now the trap itself — and the obvious version of it is wrong, which matters, because the wrong version is the one we'd reached for first, and it sends you chasing the wrong fix. You'd assume the danger of a controller that's slow to lower difficulty is that it keeps the bar too high and throws away the miner's work: shares that should have counted, rejected. That isn't what happens. The protocol freezes each job's difficulty the moment it's handed out, so lowering it later rejects nothing already in flight; and a share is worth exactly its difficulty, so a miner earns in proportion to its hashrate no matter what difficulty it's set at. There is no lost work — which is not to say no cost: a difficulty held too high still drives the connection toward the disconnect that the rest of this piece is about, and it raises payout variance. What it does not do is throw away earnings. We didn't take the protocol's word for it, either: if a too-high difficulty were quietly costing a miner its work, rejected shares would swell as it rose — we watched, and they don't. There was nothing to reclaim. (Both the protocol behavior and this measurement are derived and pinned to source in the technical paper — each job snapshots its target at issue and is validated against that snapshot until the next block.)

The real trap is about information, and it runs in a loop. When a miner's hashrate falls and the controller is slow to follow, the difficulty is now too high for what the miner can do. Too high means the miner clears it less often — so it sends fewer shares. But shares are the only thing the controller can see. Fewer shares is less to go on, so it has to wait even longer to be sure of anything — which makes it slower still to notice the hashrate is falling. Slow to notice keeps the difficulty too high, which keeps the shares sparse. Round and round.

Holding the bar too high chokes off the very shares the controller needs in order to notice the bar is too high.

The cruel part is that the opposite mistake is harmless. If the difficulty is too low, the miner clears it constantly and floods the controller with shares — more to see by, not less — so it spots the error fast and pulls itself back. One direction of error heals itself. The other digs in. That asymmetry is the entire ballgame.

And this is what the shootout had been picking up all along. The controllers that came through the declines clean weren't saving wasted work — there was none to save. They were the ones that leaned the safe way on this very asymmetry: quick to ease difficulty down onto the side that heals itself, slow to push it up toward the side that digs in. The mystery knob from the dead heat and the death spiral are the same fact approached from opposite ends — we'd stumbled on the effect, pinned it on rejected work, and only here, at the loop, is the real reason. The asymmetry helps because it keeps the controller on the side where it can still see.

So what does the spiral actually cost, if not work? It costs sight. The pool loses its read on what the miner is doing; the miner's payouts turn lumpier and more erratic as the shares thin out. And if it runs far enough, the miner reports in so rarely that the pool concludes it's gone — and drops the connection. That is the real money lost: not from a difficulty that sat too high, but from a live miner disconnected because the controller talked itself blind. Everything before that point is recoverable. The dropped connection is not.

And the "lumpier" has a size. The deeper a miner declines under a controller that won't follow it down, the noisier its payouts get — and a controller that does nothing makes them blow up fast: roughly five times noisier by the time the miner has lost most of its hashrate, while a controller that actually eases the difficulty keeps them close to normal the whole way.

Out of the box, that miner sheds most of its hashrate over a few minutes, and a controller that's slow to react never lowers the bar in time. The share rate — the dark line, the miner's heartbeat — falls straight through the disconnect floor, and the pool gives up on it. The hardware, meanwhile, has settled at a reduced level it could happily keep mining at all day. Nothing is broken. The controller dropped the connection purely by being too slow to follow the decline down — and a dropped connection is a real loss even though the miner will try to reconnect: the dead time is gone, and a controller stuck too high will keep dropping it rather than fix the cause. Drag responsiveness up and the same decline turns survivable: the heartbeat dips, then climbs back toward target as the controller eases the difficulty to match, and the miner keeps earning at the new level. Somewhere in the middle is the tipping point — and finding the setting that stays on the safe side of it, for every decline a miner can throw at you, is the whole job.

And this trap isn't hypothetical — a real, principled design walks straight into it. One well-known approach builds its vardiff on a PID controller: the standard feedback tool behind cruise control and thermostats, and a reasonable instinct, since holding a share rate steady is a regulation problem at heart. But its difficulty comes only in doublings — powers of two — so to change the setting at all, the controller has to want to move it past the halfway point between two rungs, a jump of more than 40%. At the strength it actually pushes with, a jump that large takes a swing in the share rate so big that an ordinary decline never reaches it: a third of the hashrate lost to a failing board, a fleet curtailed partway, barely moves the needle. So the difficulty sits frozen while the miner withers underneath it. It's the spiral in its purest form — not a controller merely slow to follow a decline, but one that, on the gradual declines that actually happen, can't register them as large enough to act on at all. A principled design, defeated by the exact shape of a real decline.

08 — what “better” means

Better is the controller that doesn't spiral

This is the turn the whole project took. If no controller tracks meaningfully tighter than another — they're pinned to the same precision floor — then accuracy is not a thing you can choose on — there's no real gap to pick from. But controllers do not all survive a decline equally well, and that you can choose on. So “better” stopped meaning “tracks the tightest” and started meaning “never falls into the spiral, on the declines that actually happen.”

Staying out of the spiral comes down to how long the controller waits before it believes a change. Wait too long and it's sleepy: it lags a genuine decline right into the trap. React too fast and it's twitchy: it overshoots ordinary randomness, sets the bar too high for no reason, and needlessly churns the connection. The safe setting is a valley between the two — quick enough to catch a real decline, calm enough not to invent one out of noise.

Two design choices fall straight out of the asymmetry. Be eager to lower difficulty and reluctant to raise it: lowering is the move that escapes the spiral, and when the controller must guess wrong, it should land on the harmless too-low side. And move in partial steps, so a single noisy reading can't fling the bar too high in one jump. Both are the same instinct — stay clear of the dangerous direction.

The subtle lesson came when we tried to pick the safe setting by minimizing that cost score — and couldn't. Two things we'd already established are why. The controllers all score within about 12% of each other, so there's barely any difference to minimize in the first place; and the cost everyone pictures as the dominant one — work thrown away at too-high difficulty — is actually zero, because rejected shares cost nothing. With no large, real penalty to separate the candidates, minimizing the score just ranks near-ties, and which one comes out “cheapest” slides all over the range depending on how you weight its pieces. It can't point at anything stable. What does pick the setting is a hard rule, not a number: does this controller keep a declining miner out of the spiral across every speed and severity we test — including the sparse, low-share-rate cases where it's blindest and most at risk? That question has a definite yes-or-no answer. The cost score never did.

The cost score can't choose. The safety rule can — and that's what we let make the call.

09 — the one lever

If you want more, turn up the rate

Everything so far has been about where to sit on a fixed trade-off. There is exactly one move that changes the trade-off itself — that lowers the whole curve instead of sliding along it. It isn't a cleverer algorithm. It's the target share rate: how many shares a minute you ask each miner to send.

The reason goes straight back to the floor. A controller's precision is set by how many shares it gets to count. Ask for more shares per minute and every controller sees a given change sooner and more sharply; the noise band around its estimate tightens, no matter which algorithm is underneath. This is the one knob that lowers the entire U-curve from Figure 2, and it does it for everybody at once.

The lever isn't free: more shares per minute means more messages, which costs bandwidth. This is where Stratum V2 pays off — its share messages are far smaller on the wire than the old protocol's, so V2 buys the same tightening for less traffic. It doesn't make any controller cleverer; it lowers the floor that every controller sits on.

And there's a usable band, not a single right answer. Set the rate too low and the controller goes blind to small declines — the noise swallows them; set it too high and you're paying bandwidth for precision the diminishing returns have already stopped meaningfully delivering. Where the sweet spot lands depends on the pool: how much it values steady payouts against the bandwidth it's willing to spend.

So when someone asks for a vardiff that reacts faster, the honest answer usually isn't a better controller — the field is already flat against the floor. It's turn up the share rate. You spend a little more bandwidth and a few more shares to process, and in return every miner on the pool gets sharper eyes and a tighter grip on a decline. That's the lever. It turns out there's really only the one.

10 — the refinement we tried

What about a window that follows the rate?

There's one more controller worth addressing — the one a careful reader has been assembling since Figure 2, and the most natural “cleverer algorithm” anyone proposes. If precision is the share rate times the window, and the rate is the lever, then surely the smart move is to let the window follow the rate: short while shares are plentiful, stretched out when they thin. On paper it hugs the ideal at every rate a fixed window can't.

We built it and measured it, and two things came back. The obvious recipe — stretch the window in exact inverse proportion to the rate — overshoots: it runs jumpier than it should when shares are plentiful, and the best amount of adaptation lands somewhere short of that textbook rule. But that's a footnote beside the finding that matters: however you tune it, a rate-following window only ever moves the controller's smoothness — never its safety on a decline. Across every rate we tested, letting the window chase the rate changed nothing about whether a falling miner stayed out of the spiral.

So the “smart” window lands right back where Figure 3 put everyone else: smoothness is the axis that was already a wash — the ~12% band nobody escapes — and chasing the rate with your window just slides you around inside it. That also dissolves the thing that made the idea seductive: raising the share rate and chasing it with the window are two knobs that share a word. The first lowers the floor itself, for every controller at once — that's the lever from the last section. The second only picks where you sit on a trade-off the floor has already fixed. One moves the floor; the other moves you along it, and only the first is a lever.

Raising the rate lowers the floor. Chasing the rate with your window only slides you along it — a smoothness setting, not a safer controller.

The measurements — and the precise limits of what we could resolve — live in the technical paper; and the companion build paper, “Building a vardiff,” meets the other tempting “cleverer algorithm” — estimating the decline's slope and leaning into it — and finds it fails the same way, with a new risk attached. And notice what that adds up to: the floor behind the dead heat — precision capped on a steady miner — turns out to cap a falling one too. Hand a slope-tracker the decline's exact rate for free, and it still barely follows the fall any tighter than the plain one. The same wall, reached from two directions.

11 — the two bets

Two bets on where it's hard

The two real controllers we've met each bet differently on where this problem is hard. ckpool bet on measuring — several windows blended, switched by rate — but that craft runs straight into walls we've already hit: the switching is the rate-following window of section ten (smoothness, never safety), and the precision is capped by the floor of section three. The care went where the problem doesn't reward it. The PID design bet the other way and fused the pieces into a single loop, which leaves no separate place to catch the one failure that matters — so it walks into the spiral with nothing to stop it. Neither is a bad design; both just spent their care on the wrong part. The floor settles the measuring for you, which leaves the whole fight in the deciding — and that is where we put the work.

12 — where we ended

The controller we ship

So the controller we settled on is the calmest setting that never falls into the spiral — the gentlest one that still follows every decline we tested all the way down without ever getting stuck holding the difficulty too high. It's deliberately slow, and that's the whole point: the quickness it gives up is quickness the floor would never have let it cash in, so we spent it on safety instead.

What that buys is a controller that never chokes a struggling miner. It eases off the moment the hashrate starts to fall, keeps the share stream alive so it can keep seeing, and never lets the bar sit too high while the miner withers underneath it. In hardware terms, that's the difference between a board that throttles and quietly recovers and a board that throttles and gets dropped from the pool. The measurements that back this up — and the exact limits of what we tested — live in the technical paper.

“Better vardiff” didn't mean “tracks better.” It meant: never spiral on a decline — and know that the one real lever is the share rate.

What to take away

  • Don't agonize over the algorithm. Every reasonable controller comes out about even on overall cost — they're all pinned to the same precision floor, so the choice between them is nearly a wash on accuracy.
  • Choose on the decline, not the average. Controllers differ in how they handle a falling miner, not in how tightly they track a steady one. Pick for safety on the decline, not for accuracy on the average.
  • Rejected shares aren't the enemy — a dropped connection is. Fewer shares at a too-high difficulty isn't lost earnings; it's lost visibility, and at the limit, a lost connection. Optimize against the disconnect, not the reject count.
  • Need more responsiveness? Raise the share rate. It's the only knob that lowers the floor for everyone. Everything else just trades one weakness for another.
