Build manual — the how

Building a vardiff, part by part

Start from the simplest controller that works. Take it apart. Improve each piece — and find out where improving stops mattering.

By Eric Price · Senior Software Engineer, Mara

The build, in one line:   simple average→EWMA · symmetric→asymmetric · full snap→partial step

Part two of a two-part article. This is the how — the build order. Part one, “A better vardiff,” is the why, and the place to start if you want the story of how the shape was found. (There’s also a technical report with the proofs — there if you want it, not required.)

The short version

A vardiff controller does three jobs: it measures a miner’s share rate, decides when a change is real, and moves the difficulty when it is. The plain “classic” controller mashes all three into one line of arithmetic. The way to build a good one is to pull the three apart and improve each on its own — a smoother way to measure (an EWMA), a wiser way to decide (an asymmetric sequential test), and a gentler way to move (a partial step). But there’s a lesson we’ll keep returning to: there’s really just one safety job — keeping the difficulty from running too high — and which part does it depends on the share rate. The cleverness you can spend on measuring is capped by a hard floor, and past a certain point a fancier controller doesn’t just stop helping — it starts to hurt.

01 — the starting block

The classic controller

A vardiff is the little control loop a pool runs on every connection: it nudges each miner’s difficulty up and down to keep its share rate near a chosen target — a few per minute, whether the machine is a hobby box or an industrial farm. (The companion essay, “A better vardiff,” makes the full case for why pools need this and what shape it should take; this paper assumes it.)

The controller almost everyone starts with — the one we’ll call classic — is about as simple as a control loop gets. Every so often it looks at how fast shares have been arriving, compares that to the rate it wants, and scales the difficulty by the ratio. Shares coming in twice as fast as target? Double the difficulty. Half as fast? Halve it. That’s essentially the whole thing, and it has run pools dependably for years — on a miner holding steady, which is most of them, most of the time.

We’re going to treat classic as a block of clay: the honest, working baseline we reshape into something better. To reshape it, we first have to see that the one simple line it runs is quietly doing three different jobs at once.

02 — three jobs in one

Pull the loop apart

Look closely at “scale the difficulty by the ratio of observed rate to target,” and three separate decisions are hiding inside it, each made implicitly and badly:

First, what is the rate, really? Shares don’t arrive on a metronome — they’re random, clumpy, a burst here and a gap there even from a perfectly steady miner. Turning that jittery stream into a single believable number is a measuring problem, and classic solves it with the bluntest tool available: count the shares in the last block of time and divide. Second, is this drift real, or just noise? A rate that looks 20% low for one window might be a genuine slowdown or might be three shares that happened to bunch late. Classic doesn’t ask — it reacts to whatever the last window showed. That’s a deciding problem. Third, how far should it move? Classic jumps the difficulty all the way to wherever the ratio points. That’s a moving problem.

03 — where classic hurts

It tracks a steady miner fine — and chokes a falling one

Point classic at a healthy miner holding steady and it does its job. But real miners sag — a chip throttling on heat, a failing board, a site curtailing power — drifting down over minutes, on a connection that stays up the whole time. (The companion paper, “A better vardiff,” makes the case that this gentle, partial slide — not a clean cliff — is the common case, and shows exactly why it’s the dangerous one.) The gradual slide is precisely what a slow, blunt controller handles worst.

Left to lag, classic holds the difficulty too high as the hashrate falls — which starves the miner of the very shares the controller needs to notice. That self-feeding trap is the death spiral, and the companion essay tells its full story (it even lets you watch a controller fall into one). Here we need only the upshot, because it’s the failure this whole build exists to prevent: a slow controller can lag so deep into over-difficulty that the share stream trips the pool’s disconnect line, and a live miner gets dropped for sending too little — its hardware fine the entire time.

So there are two things we might try to improve: track the rate more precisely, or never spiral on a decline. Hold onto both — we’ll work on each as we rebuild the three parts. Which one actually pays off, and which turns out to be mostly a fool’s errand, is the through-line of everything below; the answer is more lopsided than you’d expect, and the reason is a hard limit we run into in the very next part.

04 — part one · the estimator

From a plain average to a fading memory

simple moving average → EWMA (exponentially weighted moving average)

The estimator’s job is to turn the random trickle of shares into one trustworthy number for the current rate. Classic does it the obvious way: pick a window — say the last few minutes — count the shares that fell inside it, and divide by the time. A simple moving average. It works, but it has two ugly habits.

First, it has a hard edge. Every share inside the window counts fully and equally; the instant a share ages past the window’s far edge, it counts for nothing. So a burst of shares enters the window all at once, rides at full weight for the whole window length, and then — one window-length later — all drops out together as the clump crosses the trailing edge in a single step. The estimate lurches at that exit, not because the miner changed, but because the window swept the whole burst out at once. Second, it forces a bad either-or: a short window is quick to react but jumpy (few shares, lots of noise); a long window is calm but sluggish (it keeps averaging in old, stale data long after things have changed).

The upgrade is a small idea with a clumsy name: the exponentially weighted moving average, or EWMA. Instead of a hard window, keep one running estimate and, each time a fresh reading comes in, nudge it a little of the way toward that reading:

new estimate  =  (a little of) the latest reading  +  (mostly) the old estimate

That one line quietly fixes both habits. Old data doesn’t drop off a cliff — it fades, each reading counting a bit less than the one after it, smoothly into the past. And there’s no window to choose anymore, just a single dial: how big “a little” is. A big nudge means the estimate is mostly the latest reading — quick, but jumpy. A small nudge means it leans on its long memory — calm, but slow. One knob, sliding between the same two extremes the window forced on us, but smoothly and without the edge.

And here is the catch that the companion paper makes its centerpiece, so we’ll only state it: that one dial can’t be cheated. How many shares you’ve gathered sets a hard floor on precision — a longer memory is steadier but laggier, a shorter one quicker but noisier, and the budget you’re spending between them is fixed by the share rate, not by how clever the estimator is. No estimator gets under that floor; a simple average and an EWMA tuned to the same memory sit at essentially the same point on it. (“A better vardiff” draws this exact trade, and the floor beneath it, in full.) So choose the EWMA for what it genuinely gives — smoothness, one honest dial, no edge effects — not in any hope of out-measuring the floor. That hope is the first thing the next two parts will teach you to drop.

05 — part two · the trigger

From snap judgments to weighing evidence — then a thumb on the scale

react to each window → accumulate evidence (a CUSUM) → make it lopsided

The estimator hands us a number. The trigger’s job is to decide whether that number has drifted far enough from target to actually move the difficulty. This part comes in two upgrades, and the second is where this build’s safety comes from — at least while shares are plentiful (Section 7 has the rest of that story).

Upgrade A — stop judging each window alone

Classic decides fresh every check-in: is the rate off right now? If so, move. The problem is that a single window is a noisy witness. Demand a big drift before acting and you’re slow to catch a real change; act on a small one and you’ll fire constantly on noise that means nothing. Either way you’re throwing away something you already have — the history. A rate that reads a little low this window and a little low last window and a little low the window before is telling you something three separate snap-judgments each shrug off.

The fix is a method called a CUSUM — short for “cumulative sum,” which is exactly what it is. Instead of asking “is the rate off now?” keep a running tally of evidence. Every window the rate looks low, add to the tally; every window it looks high, subtract. One-off noise pushes the tally up and down around zero and cancels out. But a genuine, persistent drift pushes it the same direction over and over, and the tally climbs steadily until it crosses a line — and that is when you fire. A small change you couldn’t trust in any one window becomes undeniable once its evidence has piled up. You react to the weight of evidence, not the last glance.

Upgrade B — the two directions are not equal

So far we’ve made the trigger wiser, not safer. The safety upgrade is one fact: raising the difficulty too far and lowering it too far are not equally dangerous. The companion essay spends its heart on why; here we take that as given and put it to work.

Set the bar too low and the error is benign and self-correcting; set it too high and you’ve taken the first step of the spiral from Section 3. One side heals, the other digs in — so the trigger shouldn’t weigh them the same. Make it eager to ease (quick to lower difficulty on the slightest sign the miner is fading) and reluctant to tighten (demanding much stronger evidence before it raises the bar): a small thumb on the scale, always pressing toward the safe side. This one-sided reluctance is what separated the good controllers from the bad. The obvious reason for it — that easing fast “saves wasted work” — is a red herring; the companion essay shows there’s no wasted work to save. Here we don’t re-argue that — we just build the asymmetry in.

It costs almost nothing on a healthy miner and it is decisive on a failing one. When the hashrate sags, an eager-to-ease trigger lowers the bar fast enough to keep the share stream alive — so it keeps seeing, and never enters the spiral. The reluctant-to-tighten half guards the same edge from the other direction: when the trigger is slow to raise the bar and the miner was actually fine, it errs into the harmless too-low side, never the dangerous too-high one.

Safety isn’t a part you bolt on. It’s a job — protecting the over-difficulty side — and which part holds it shifts with the share rate.

06 — part three · the step

From a full snap to a partial move — and a myth to retire

jump all the way → move part of the way

The trigger has decided to move. The last job is how far. Classic snaps the difficulty all the way to wherever the new estimate points. But the estimate is never perfect — it carries the noise we met in Part 1 — so a full jump chases that noise. Overshoot one way, then the next reading pulls it back the other, and the difficulty hunts back and forth around the right value instead of settling on it.

The upgrade is simply to move part of the way each time — a partial step. Take a fraction of the distance to where the estimate says, let the next reading refine it, take another fraction. It converges a little more slowly, but it glides into place instead of overshooting and ringing. One dial again: a big step is fast but bouncy; a small step is calm but unhurried.

And one thing the step pointedly does not need: any safety logic of its own. You might expect raising the difficulty to be the costly direction — throwing away the partly-finished work aimed at the old, lower bar — which would make the step lopsided like the trigger. It isn’t: a share is validated against the difficulty it was issued at, so moving the bar rejects nothing already in flight. Raising and lowering are equally free. (The essay runs this down with the receipts — it even watches the rejected-share stream to confirm there’s nothing there to reclaim.) So the step is the one part with no safety role at all: choose it for the settling in Figure 6, and nothing more.

07 — assembling it

Who keeps the miner safe changes with the share rate

Snap the three upgraded parts together and you almost have it — but first, a wiring detail the “assembly line” picture quietly hides. The pipeline mostly runs one way: measure, decide, move. The exception turns on something you already know — a higher bar means sparser shares, since a miner clears a harder target less often. So the instant the step raises the difficulty, the share rate drops — not because the miner faltered, but because the controller just moved the goalposts. An estimator that isn’t told will read that dip as the miner fading. So the step sends one signal back every time it fires: that drop is me, not the miner. Pulling the three jobs apart didn’t make them independent — it left exactly one wire running backward, the unavoidable price of measuring something you’re also moving, now named and in the open instead of buried inside a single loop.

The other assembly subtlety is the prettier one: which part is responsible for safety depends on how many shares are coming in.

At a healthy share rate, the trigger carries it. Enough evidence is flowing that the asymmetric, evidence-weighing trigger from Part 2 can do its job — tell a real decline from noise and lean to the safe side. But when shares are sparse — a small miner, or one already partway into a decline — the trigger runs out of the one thing it needs. With so few readings arriving, there simply isn’t enough evidence to accumulate, and the sequential test can no longer function: it quietly collapses back into a plain, symmetric trigger — the kind from before Part 2, with no directional caution at all. Right where a falling miner is most fragile, the part that was supposed to protect it goes neutral.

So at sparse rates the safety job hands off to the estimator — and this is where its slowness, the apparent weakness from Part 1, turns out to be the point. A deliberately slow estimator’s belief moves so gently that it simply cannot lurch upward fast enough to cross the bar and fire a tightening move. It doesn’t protect the miner by leaning to the safe side; it protects the miner by being too inert to make the dangerous move at all. So the over-difficulty direction is guarded either way — by the trigger’s asymmetry where shares are plentiful enough for it to work, and by the estimator’s sluggishness where they aren’t — with the handoff falling at a chosen share-rate seam.

Two things show up immediately. The upgrade that decides whether the connection lives or dies is the asymmetric trigger — flip it on a deep decline and a drop becomes a ride. The EWMA estimator, by contrast, only steadies the share trace (watch the jitter smooth out when you switch it on); turn it on under the symmetric trigger and the miner still drops, exactly as the floor demands — a better estimator can’t get you under it, so it can’t save a steep fall on its own. And the step? It changes how the share rate settles — full snap overshoots and ripples, partial glides — but never whether the miner survives. Two caveats in fairness: the demo is schematic (the gaps between builds are drawn larger than life for clarity), and the step’s safety-neutrality here is built in rather than discovered. But the three directions it teaches are the real ones — trigger for life-or-death, estimator for steadiness, step for comfort.

09 — knowing when to stop

The upgrade you should not make

With the three parts in place and the seam handled, the controller is good — and the theory paper says it’s about as good as anything can be, because it’s sitting on the precision floor. That’s exactly when the temptation to keep going is strongest, so it’s worth naming the next upgrade and why to refuse it.

The obvious move is a cleverer estimator. Our EWMA only tracks the level of the rate; why not also track its slope — notice that the hashrate is trending down and lean into the trend, reacting before the level has even finished falling? It sounds like a clean win, and we built one to check.

It is not a win, for two reasons that compound. First, the gain barely exists. Handed the decline’s true slope for free, a trend-aware tracker beat the plain one by only about five percent — and a real one, which has to learn the slope from the same sparse, noisy shares, clawed back almost none of even that. The floor isn’t quite untouchable on a decline; it’s just that slipping under it buys a sliver, and only if you cheat. Second, and worse: a slope estimate is a jittery thing on sparse data. A run of unlucky readings can make the trend estimate swing positive — the tracker briefly believes the miner is rising — and on that belief it fires a tightening move, raising the bar on a miner that’s actually falling. That is the precise first step of the spiral, the one move Section 7’s slow estimator was safe because it structurally could not make. So the clever estimator reintroduces, right in the sparse corner where it’s most dangerous, the exact failure we spent the whole build engineering out — in exchange for an improvement the floor guarantees is negligible. And it isn’t the only “cleverer algorithm” that fails this way: the companion paper takes up the other natural one — letting the averaging window chase the share rate — and finds the same thing, that it moves only smoothness, never safety. Two different roads to sophistication, the same dead end.

Past the floor, “more sophisticated” doesn’t mean better. It means a smaller gain and a bigger risk.

10 — the build

The recipe, in order

Put it all together and the controller worth shipping is short to describe — and most of its wisdom is in what it deliberately doesn’t do.

How to build it

  1. Measure with an EWMA, set slow. One memory dial, no hard window. Don’t pour effort into precision — the floor caps it, and a slow estimate is part of how you stay safe.
  2. Decide with an asymmetric sequential test. Accumulate evidence rather than reacting to each window; then put a thumb on the scale — eager to ease, reluctant to tighten. This is what protects the over-difficulty side while shares are plentiful — spend your care here.
  3. Move with a partial step. One gain dial, for smoothness only. Raising and lowering are equally cheap, so the step needs no safety logic — just don’t let it overshoot.
  4. Hand off safety across a share-rate seam. Let the asymmetric trigger guard when shares are plentiful; below the seam, neutralize it and let the slow estimator — too gentle to be spooked — carry the safe direction.
  5. Then stop. Don’t add slope-chasing or other cleverness. Past the floor it can’t help, and it re-opens the spiral exactly where data is thinnest.
  6. Need more responsiveness? Raise the share rate. It’s the only knob that lifts the whole field off the floor — the companion paper’s one true lever. Everything else just trades one weakness for another.

The shape of it

Building a good vardiff isn’t a search for a clever algorithm — it’s separating three jobs, doing two of them simply, and concentrating all the real care on the one that keeps a failing miner from being choked off and dropped. The classic controller wasn’t wrong so much as fused: it couldn’t put its care where the danger is, because it never told the danger apart from the routine. Pull the pieces apart, and the right design almost falls out on its own.
